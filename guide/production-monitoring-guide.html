The core principle

// Analogy

Think of observability like the instrument panel in a cockpit. The pilot doesn't watch every wire and fuel line directly — they read instruments. Logs are the flight recorder (what happened), metrics are the gauges (how things are right now), and traces are the flight path (how you got here).

✅
Rule of thumb

Alert on metrics → investigate with logs → confirm with traces. These three pillars serve different purposes and work best together, not as substitutes for each other.

Chapter 01 — Foundations

Why Monitoring Matters

The four golden signals and the three pillars that underpin every observability decision you'll ever make.

Four Golden Signals Three Pillars SRE Observability

You can't fix what you can't see. Monitoring is not about collecting data — it's about having the right data at the moment something goes wrong, before a customer tells you about it.

The four golden signals

Google's Site Reliability Engineering book defines four signals that, between them, tell you almost everything you need to know about a production system's health.

⏱️
Latency

How long requests take to complete. Track successful requests separately from failed ones — a slow 200 is often as damaging as a 500. Always use p50, p95, p99 — never averages.

🚦
Traffic

Requests per second, throughput, active sessions. This is your demand signal. Traffic trends establish your baseline — without it, you can't tell normal from abnormal.

💥
Errors

The rate of failed requests — 5xx HTTP codes, uncaught exceptions, failed DB operations, null pointer exceptions. Both explicit failures and implicit ones (wrong results).

🪣
Saturation

How "full" your system is. CPU %, memory %, disk I/O, thread pool exhaustion, DB connection pool size. Systems degrade before they break — saturation is the early warning.

⚠️
Never use averages for latency

An average of 100ms can hide that 5% of users experience 2000ms. Use percentiles: p50 (median), p95 (95th percentile), p99. Your worst users matter. Alert on p99, not mean.

The three pillars of observability

L
Logs — What happened
Structured text events emitted during execution. Logs answer "why" — they contain the full context of an event. Best for debugging specific incidents once you know something went wrong. Use structured JSON, not plain strings.
M
Metrics — How things are right now
Numeric time-series data sampled at regular intervals. Cheap to store, fast to query, great for alerting. Metrics tell you "that" something is wrong. They're the trigger for investigation — not the investigation itself.
T
Traces — How you got here
End-to-end journeys across services. A trace is composed of spans — one per unit of work (HTTP call, DB query, cache lookup). Traces show where latency lives across service boundaries. Essential in microservices.
// Analogy

Think of a hospital: metrics are the vital signs monitor beeping at the nurse station (tells you something is wrong), logs are the patient's medical notes (tell you the history and why), and traces are the patient's journey — ER → imaging → surgery → recovery — showing where time was spent.

"Alert on metrics, investigate with logs, confirm with traces."

💡
Start here, not with tools

Before choosing Prometheus vs Datadog or ELK vs Loki, get the four signals right and structure your logs as JSON. Tool choice is secondary — good observability habits come first.

JVM internals

♻️
GC Pause Times

GC pauses >500ms cause visible latency spikes. Watch G1GC pause time, GC frequency, and heap reclaimed per cycle.

🧵
Thread States

Monitor BLOCKED, WAITING, and TIMED_WAITING thread counts. A deadlock shows up as BLOCKED threads piling up.

🔌
Connection Pools

DB and HTTP connection pool utilization. Near-full pools cause request queuing. HikariCP exposes these via Micrometer.

Business KPIs — the most important layer

Infrastructure metrics tell you the system is struggling. Business metrics tell you the business is hurting. These are the signals your stakeholders actually care about.

1
Transaction success rate
Orders placed per minute, payment success rate, checkout completion rate. A drop here is a revenue signal that no CPU chart captures.
2
User funnel completion
Signup rate, login success rate, onboarding completion. A drop in signups with healthy CPU often means a third-party auth provider is down.
3
Queue / async job health
Consumer lag (Kafka), dead letter queue depth, job processing time. A growing DLQ means data is being lost silently.
4
External dependency health
HTTP call latency and error rate to third-party APIs, circuit breaker state, timeout rate. Third-party failures cascade unless isolated.

✅
Start with what your business cares about

Every metric you add has a maintenance cost. Prioritize metrics that trigger action. A metric that nobody acts on when it alerts is noise. A business KPI that drops 10% without anyone noticing is a gap in your observability.

Chapter 03 — The Three Pillars

Centralized Logging

Aggregating logs from every server into one place — shippers, structured JSON, and the ELK / Loki pipeline.

FilebeatFluentdElasticsearch LogstashKibanaLoki LogbackJSON logs

Logs written to a single server are useful during development. In production with multiple servers, they're useless without centralization — you can't manually SSH into 20 servers to grep a log file.

The centralized logging pipeline

1
App writes structured JSON logs to stdout / file
Never ship logs from inside your application. Write JSON to stdout or a rolling file. Let an agent handle delivery. This decouples your app from the log destination.
2
Log shipper on each server tails and forwards
Filebeat (Elastic), Fluentd / Fluent Bit (flexible, cloud-native), or Promtail (Grafana Loki). Each instance runs on every server, reads logs, and ships to the central collector.
3
Logstash / Kafka acts as a buffer and transformer
Logstash parses, filters, and enriches log events. Kafka is often used as an intermediate buffer to absorb spikes without losing data when the downstream is slow.
4
Elasticsearch / Loki stores and indexes
Elasticsearch indexes every field for full-text search. Loki stores compressed log chunks indexed only by labels — cheaper but less flexible. Choose based on query patterns.
5
Kibana / Grafana for querying and dashboards
Kibana's Discover for Elasticsearch, Grafana's Explore panel for Loki. Both let you filter by time, service, severity, and any field in your JSON log.

Why structured JSON logging

A plain text log line like 2024-01-15 ERROR Failed to process order 12345 requires a regex to parse. If the format changes, the regex breaks silently. JSON logs are parseable without fragile patterns.

MDC Correlation Injecting correlation ID into every log line automatically 
import org.slf4j.MDC;

@Component
public class CorrelationIdFilter implements Filter {

    private static final String CORRELATION_HEADER = "X-Correlation-ID";
    private static final String MDC_KEY = "correlationId";

    @Override
    public void doFilter(ServletRequest req, ServletResponse res,
                          FilterChain chain) throws IOException, ServletException {
        HttpServletRequest httpReq = (HttpServletRequest) req;
        HttpServletResponse httpRes = (HttpServletResponse) res;

        String correlationId = httpReq.getHeader(CORRELATION_HEADER);
        if (correlationId == null || correlationId.isBlank()) {
            correlationId = UUID.randomUUID().toString();
        }

        // Inject into MDC — every subsequent log call gets this field
        MDC.put(MDC_KEY, correlationId);
        // Echo back so clients can trace their request
        httpRes.setHeader(CORRELATION_HEADER, correlationId);

        try {
            chain.doFilter(req, res);
        } finally {
            MDC.remove(MDC_KEY); // ALWAYS clean up — thread pool reuse
        }
    }
}
Servlet Filter Enriching MDC with user context from JWT / SecurityContext 
@Component
@Order(2) // runs after CorrelationIdFilter
public class UserContextFilter implements Filter {

    @Override
    public void doFilter(ServletRequest req, ServletResponse res,
                          FilterChain chain) throws IOException, ServletException {
        try {
            Authentication auth = SecurityContextHolder.getContext().getAuthentication();
            if (auth != null && auth.isAuthenticated()) {
                MDC.put("userId", auth.getName());
                MDC.put("roles", auth.getAuthorities().toString());
            }
            chain.doFilter(req, res);
        } finally {
            MDC.remove("userId");
            MDC.remove("roles");
        }
    }
}

ELK vs Loki — how to choose

Elasticsearch indexes every JSON field — powerful full-text search but expensive at scale. Loki only indexes labels (service, env, level) and compresses the log body — much cheaper, but you can only filter by those labels before scanning. Choose ELK if you need ad-hoc field queries; Loki if you query mostly by service + time range.

Metrics & Alerting

Metrics are cheap to store and fast to query — they're the first line of defence. The goal is not to collect everything, but to collect the right things and have the right alerts fire before customers notice.

How Prometheus works

// Analogy

Prometheus is a polling system — it reaches out to each service on a schedule and asks "what are your numbers right now?". This is called scraping. Your app exposes an HTTP endpoint (usually /actuator/prometheus) and Prometheus calls it every 15 seconds. Think of it as a health inspector making rounds.

Counter Counting business events — orders placed, payments failed 
@Service
public class OrderService {

    private final Counter ordersPlaced;
    private final Counter paymentsFailed;

    public OrderService(MeterRegistry registry) {
        // Tags partition metrics — query by region, product type, etc.
        this.ordersPlaced = Counter.builder("orders.placed")
            .description("Total orders successfully placed")
            .tag("region", "us-east")
            .register(registry);

        this.paymentsFailed = Counter.builder("payments.failed")
            .description("Payment processing failures")
            .tag("reason", "unknown")
            .register(registry);
    }

    public Order placeOrder(OrderRequest request) {
        try {
            Order order = processOrder(request);
            ordersPlaced.increment();
            return order;
        } catch (PaymentException e) {
            paymentsFailed.increment();
            throw e;
        }
    }
}
Timer Measuring latency — p50, p95, p99 automatically computed 
@Service
public class PaymentService {

    private final Timer paymentTimer;

    public PaymentService(MeterRegistry registry) {
        this.paymentTimer = Timer.builder("payment.processing.time")
            .description("Time to process a payment")
            .publishPercentiles(0.5, 0.95, 0.99) // p50, p95, p99
            .publishPercentileHistogram()
            .register(registry);
    }

    public PaymentResult process(Payment payment) {
        return paymentTimer.record(() -> callPaymentGateway(payment));
    }

    // Or use the annotation — Micrometer instruments the method automatically
    @Timed(value = "payment.refund.time", percentiles = {0.95, 0.99})
    public RefundResult refund(String orderId) {
        return callRefundGateway(orderId);
    }
}

# prometheus-alerts.yml
groups:
  - name: application
    rules:
      - alert: HighErrorRate
        expr: |
          rate(http_server_requests_seconds_count{status=~"5.."}[5m])
          /
          rate(http_server_requests_seconds_count[5m]) > 0.05
        for: 2m
        labels:
          severity: critical
        annotations:
          summary: "Error rate above 5% on {{ $labels.instance }}"

      - alert: HighLatencyP99
        expr: |
          histogram_quantile(0.99,
            rate(http_server_requests_seconds_bucket[5m])
          ) > 2.0
        for: 3m
        labels:
          severity: warning
        annotations:
          summary: "p99 latency above 2s"

      - alert: QueueDepthHigh
        expr: task_queue_size > 500
        for: 5m
        labels:
          severity: warning
        annotations:
          summary: "Task queue has {{ $value }} pending items"
💡
The for: clause is critical

Without for: 2m, every momentary spike fires an alert. The for clause requires the condition to be true continuously before alerting. This dramatically reduces noise from transient spikes.

Chapter 05 — The Three Pillars

Distributed Tracing

Correlation IDs, W3C traceparent propagation, and tracing a user across a stateless web tier with OpenTelemetry.

Correlation IDMDCtraceparent OpenTelemetryJaegerZipkinTempo

In a stateless web tier, a user's Request 1 hits Server A and Request 2 hits Server B. Without a shared identifier threaded through every log line and HTTP call, these two requests are invisible to each other — and debugging a user's session becomes a nightmare.

The correlation ID pattern

1
Generate at the entry point
When a request first enters your system (API Gateway, load balancer, or first service), generate a UUID. This becomes the correlation ID for the entire request chain — even across multiple downstream services.
2
Inject into MDC immediately
MDC (Mapped Diagnostic Context) is a per-thread key-value store built into SLF4J. Every subsequent log.info(), log.error() call on that thread automatically includes every MDC key. Zero boilerplate on the caller side.
3
Propagate on every outgoing HTTP call
Use a RestTemplate interceptor or WebClient filter to add X-Correlation-ID (or W3C traceparent) to every outgoing HTTP call. Each downstream service extracts it and repeats steps 1–3.
4
Query logs by that ID
In Kibana or Grafana, search correlationId:"f47ac10b-58cc" and see every log line from every server, in order, for that single request chain. Instant cross-server debugging.

// Correlation ID propagation
RestTemplate Interceptor that adds X-Correlation-ID to every outgoing HTTP call 
@Component
public class CorrelationIdInterceptor implements ClientHttpRequestInterceptor {

    @Override
    public ClientHttpResponse intercept(HttpRequest request,
            byte[] body, ClientHttpRequestExecution execution) throws IOException {

        String correlationId = MDC.get("correlationId");
        if (correlationId != null) {
            request.getHeaders().add("X-Correlation-ID", correlationId);
        }
        return execution.execute(request, body);
    }
}

@Configuration
public class RestTemplateConfig {

    @Bean
    public RestTemplate restTemplate(CorrelationIdInterceptor interceptor) {
        RestTemplate template = new RestTemplate();
        template.setInterceptors(List.of(interceptor));
        return template;
    }
}
WebClient Reactive WebClient filter for correlation ID propagation 
@Configuration
public class WebClientConfig {

    @Bean
    public WebClient webClient() {
        return WebClient.builder()
            .filter((request, next) -> {
                String correlationId = MDC.get("correlationId");
                ClientRequest mutated = ClientRequest.from(request)
                    .header("X-Correlation-ID",
                        correlationId != null ? correlationId : "unknown")
                    .build();
                return next.exchange(mutated);
            })
            .build();
    }
}

Async Threads MDC does NOT cross thread boundaries — explicit propagation required 
// PROBLEM: MDC is ThreadLocal — async tasks lose it
@Async
public void processAsync(String orderId) {
    // MDC is EMPTY here — correlationId is lost!
    log.info("Processing {}", orderId);
}

// SOLUTION 1: Pass correlationId explicitly
@Async
public void processAsync(String orderId, String correlationId) {
    MDC.put("correlationId", correlationId);
    try {
        log.info("Processing {}", orderId); // now has correlationId
    } finally { MDC.clear(); }
}

// SOLUTION 2: MDC-aware task decorator on the thread pool
@Bean
public Executor asyncExecutor() {
    ThreadPoolTaskExecutor executor = new ThreadPoolTaskExecutor();
    executor.setTaskDecorator(runnable -> {
        Map<String, String> mdcContext = MDC.getCopyOfContextMap();
        return () -> {
            if (mdcContext != null) MDC.setContextMap(mdcContext);
            try { runnable.run(); } finally { MDC.clear(); }
        };
    });
    executor.initialize();
    return executor;
}
⚠️
MDC is ThreadLocal — async is the #1 gotcha

MDC data lives on the current thread. The moment you use @Async, CompletableFuture, or a Kafka consumer on a different thread pool, the MDC map is empty. Always use a task decorator or pass the context explicitly. Micrometer Tracing handles this automatically for instrumented operations.

Custom Health HealthIndicator for an external dependency — payment gateway 
@Component
public class PaymentGatewayHealthIndicator implements HealthIndicator {

    private final PaymentGatewayClient client;

    public PaymentGatewayHealthIndicator(PaymentGatewayClient client) {
        this.client = client;
    }

    @Override
    public Health health() {
        try {
            long start = System.currentTimeMillis();
            boolean reachable = client.ping();
            long latency = System.currentTimeMillis() - start;

            if (reachable) {
                return Health.up()
                    .withDetail("latencyMs", latency)
                    .withDetail("gateway", "stripe")
                    .build();
            }
            return Health.down().withDetail("reason", "ping failed").build();
        } catch (Exception e) {
            return Health.down(e).build();
        }
    }
}

Actuator Security Lock down sensitive endpoints — expose /prometheus only to Prometheus 
@Configuration
public class ActuatorSecurityConfig {

    @Bean
    public SecurityFilterChain actuatorSecurity(HttpSecurity http) throws Exception {
        http
            .securityMatcher("/actuator/**")
            .authorizeHttpRequests(auth -> auth
                // /health and /info are safe to expose publicly
                .requestMatchers("/actuator/health/**").permitAll()
                .requestMatchers("/actuator/info").permitAll()
                // /prometheus — restrict to monitoring network / IP
                .requestMatchers("/actuator/prometheus")
                    .hasIpAddress("10.0.0.0/8") // internal only
                // Everything else requires admin role
                .anyRequest().hasRole("ACTUATOR_ADMIN")
            );
        return http.build();
    }

Cloud Logging, Cloud Monitoring, Cloud Trace, Cloud Profiler — GCP's native observability suite and how it maps to open-source alternatives.

Cloud LoggingCloud MonitoringCloud Trace Cloud ProfilerError ReportingSynthetic Monitoring

GCP's Operations suite (formerly Stackdriver) covers all three observability pillars natively. If you're deploying to GKE or Cloud Run, the default setup auto-collects a significant amount — you mostly configure, not build.

GCP Observability — the full picture

📜
Cloud Logging

Centralized log management. Auto-collects from GKE, GCE, Cloud Run, Cloud Functions. Supports log-based metrics (turn log patterns into alertable metrics). Query with Logs Explorer using a powerful filter language similar to SQL.

📊
Cloud Monitoring

Metrics collection, dashboards (MQL), and uptime alerting. Integrates natively with all GCP services. Push custom metrics from Spring Boot via Micrometer's Cloud Monitoring exporter. Includes uptime checks (equivalent to Site24x7 pings).

🔗
Cloud Trace

Distributed tracing integrated with OpenTelemetry. Shows request latency breakdowns per span across GKE pods and Cloud Run. Displays Gantt charts of service call hierarchies. Correlates with logs via trace ID.

⚡
Cloud Profiler

Continuous CPU and memory profiling in production. Very low overhead (<1% CPU). Generates flame graphs. Invaluable for identifying hot code paths and memory allocation sites in Java/Spring apps without a production slowdown.

🚨
Error Reporting

Automatically groups and surfaces exceptions from Cloud Logging. Sends alerts on new error types. Shows error frequency over time. Saves you from manually grepping logs for NullPointerExceptions.

🧪
Synthetic Monitoring

Runs Puppeteer-based scripts that simulate user journeys (click, fill form, check response) from multiple regions globally. GCP's equivalent of Site24x7 web transaction monitors. GA since 2023.

<!-- pom.xml -->
<dependency>
  <groupId>com.google.cloud</groupId>
  <artifactId>spring-cloud-gcp-starter-logging</artifactId>
</dependency>

Chapter 08 — Reference

Concepts & Tools

Every tool, framework, and concept mentioned in this guide — explained in one place.

The three pillars

Logs

Structured text events emitted during execution. Best for debugging why something happened. Should be JSON in production.

Metrics

Numeric time-series data sampled at intervals. Cheap to store, fast to query. Best for alerting and dashboards.

Traces

End-to-end request journeys composed of spans. Show latency across service boundaries. Essential in microservices.

Span

A single unit of work within a trace — one HTTP call, one DB query, one cache lookup. Has a start time and duration.

Trace ID

A unique identifier shared by all spans in a single request chain, across all services and servers.

Correlation ID

A simpler per-request UUID threaded through logs and HTTP headers. A manual, log-based version of a trace ID.

Logging tools

SLF4J

Logging facade for Java. Logger.info(), Logger.error() etc. Actual logging done by Logback or Log4j2 behind the scenes.

Logback

Default logging implementation in Spring Boot. Configured via logback-spring.xml. Supports async appenders and JSON encoding.

MDC

Mapped Diagnostic Context. Per-thread key-value map in SLF4J. Keys are auto-injected into every log line on that thread.

logstash-logback-encoder

Logback encoder that outputs each log event as a JSON object. Essential for structured logging to Elasticsearch/Loki.

Filebeat

Lightweight log shipper from Elastic. Runs as an agent on each server, tails log files, ships to Logstash or Elasticsearch.

Fluentd / Fluent Bit

Open-source log shippers. Fluent Bit is lighter and better for cloud-native / container environments. Both support many output plugins.

Promtail

Grafana's log shipper designed specifically for Loki. Like Filebeat but for the Loki ecosystem.

Logstash

Log pipeline tool. Receives, parses, filters, and enriches log events before forwarding to Elasticsearch. The "L" in ELK.

Elasticsearch

Distributed search and analytics engine. Indexes every JSON field for full-text search. The "E" in ELK stack.

Kibana

Web UI for querying and visualizing Elasticsearch data. The "K" in ELK. Used for log search, dashboards, alerting.

Grafana Loki

Log aggregation system that only indexes labels (service, env). Much cheaper than Elasticsearch but less powerful for ad-hoc queries.

Metrics tools

Micrometer

Metrics facade for JVM apps. Like SLF4J but for metrics. Supports backends: Prometheus, Datadog, CloudWatch, Influx, etc.

Counter

Micrometer meter that only goes up. For counting events: orders placed, emails sent, exceptions thrown.

Timer

Micrometer meter for latency measurement. Records count and sum. Computes p50/p95/p99 automatically.

Gauge

Micrometer meter for values that go up and down. For queue depth, active connections, cache size.

Prometheus

Open-source metrics database. Scrapes HTTP endpoints on a schedule. Stores time-series data. PromQL for queries.

PromQL

Prometheus Query Language. Used in alert rules and Grafana panels. rate(), histogram_quantile(), label_replace() are key functions.

Grafana

Open-source dashboarding. Connects to Prometheus, Loki, Elasticsearch, Cloud Monitoring and many more. The standard visualization layer.

Alertmanager

Prometheus's alerting component. Routes alerts to Slack, PagerDuty, OpsGenie. Handles deduplication, silencing, and grouping.

Tracing tools

OpenTelemetry

Vendor-neutral observability framework covering logs, metrics, and traces. The industry standard. Instrument once, export anywhere.

Micrometer Tracing

Spring Boot 3.x tracing abstraction (replaces Spring Cloud Sleuth). Bridges to OpenTelemetry or Brave (Zipkin).

Spring Cloud Sleuth

Older Spring tracing library (pre-Boot 3.x). Auto-instruments Spring components. Now superseded by Micrometer Tracing.

Jaeger

Open-source distributed tracing backend from Uber. Stores and visualizes traces. Compatible with OpenTelemetry exporter.

Zipkin

Open-source tracing backend. Older than Jaeger, still widely used. Compatible with OpenTelemetry Zipkin exporter.

Grafana Tempo

Grafana's distributed tracing backend. Cost-efficient (no indexing), works seamlessly with Loki for log correlation.

W3C traceparent

Standard HTTP header for distributed trace context propagation. Format: version-traceId-parentSpanId-flags. Understood by all OTEL-compatible systems.

X-Correlation-ID

Custom HTTP header convention for passing a request correlation ID. Simpler than traceparent but not standardized. Use traceparent when possible.

Spring Boot tooling

Spring Boot Actuator

Auto-configures HTTP endpoints for health checks, metrics, log level management, thread dumps, and environment inspection.

HealthIndicator

Interface to implement for custom health checks. Registered automatically in /actuator/health. Return Health.up() or Health.down().

MeterRegistry

Micrometer's central registry. Inject it into any Spring bean to register Counters, Timers, Gauges. Auto-configured by Spring Boot.

@Timed

Micrometer annotation that wraps a method in a Timer automatically. Requires the TimedAspect bean to be registered.

HikariCP

Default Spring Boot JDBC connection pool. Exposes active, idle, pending, max connection counts as Micrometer metrics automatically.

GCP services

Cloud Logging

GCP's centralized log management. Auto-collects from GKE, Cloud Run, GCE. Logs Explorer for querying. Supports log-based metrics.

Cloud Monitoring

GCP metrics, dashboards, and alerting. Includes uptime checks. Accepts custom metrics via Micrometer Stackdriver exporter.

Cloud Trace

GCP's distributed tracing. Integrates with OpenTelemetry. Correlates with Cloud Logging via trace ID in log entries.

Cloud Profiler

Continuous production CPU/memory profiling. Low overhead. Generates flame graphs. Best way to find hot code paths in Java.

Error Reporting

Auto-groups exceptions from Cloud Logging. Sends alerts on new error types. Shows error trends over time.

Synthetic Monitoring

Puppeteer-based scripts that simulate user journeys from multiple global regions. GCP's equivalent of Site24x7 web transaction monitoring.

Chapter 09 — Reference

Interview Prep

Questions that come up in senior and staff engineering rounds — with model answers that show depth, not just definitions.

Foundational "What are the three pillars of observability and how do they differ?"

The interviewer wants to know if you understand these as distinct tools with different purposes — not just buzz words.

  • Logs are for debugging why — full event context, expensive to search at scale
  • Metrics are for detecting that — cheap time-series, fast to query, basis for alerting
  • Traces are for locating where — latency across service call chains
  • They complement, not replace, each other — metrics trigger, logs explain, traces locate
The three pillars serve different questions. Metrics tell you that something is wrong — your error rate spiked. Logs tell you why — the stack trace and context around the event. Traces tell you where — which service in a chain of calls is taking 2 seconds. In practice, a Prometheus alert fires first, you open Grafana to see the spike, then grep logs by correlation ID to find the root cause, then look at traces to confirm which downstream call is the bottleneck. Each one leads you to the next.
Weak answer: listing definitions without connecting them to how you'd use them together during an actual incident.
Foundational "Why should you use percentiles instead of averages for latency metrics?"

Tests whether you understand how averages mask tail latency — a very common production monitoring mistake.

  • Averages are easily skewed by a large number of fast requests hiding slow outliers
  • p99 tells you what 1 in 100 users actually experiences
  • SLAs are almost always percentile-based ("99% of requests under 500ms")
  • In practice, slow users are often premium customers or users with large accounts
If you have 1000 requests: 990 take 10ms and 10 take 5000ms, the average is about 60ms — looks totally fine. But p99 is 5000ms — 1% of users are waiting 5 seconds. Those might be your highest-value customers running large batch operations. Averages are actively misleading for latency. I always configure Micrometer with publishPercentiles(0.5, 0.95, 0.99) and alert on p99, not mean.
Only mentioning that averages "aren't accurate" without giving a concrete example of why they deceive you.
Intermediate "In a stateless web tier where requests go to different servers, how do you trace a user's request across servers in your logs?"

Tests practical distributed systems knowledge — correlation IDs and MDC are the expected answer.

  • Generate a correlation ID (UUID) at the first entry point
  • Store it in MDC so every log line on that thread carries it automatically
  • Propagate it via HTTP header (X-Correlation-ID or W3C traceparent) on all outgoing calls
  • Watch out for async — MDC is ThreadLocal, must be copied explicitly across thread boundaries
We use a servlet filter that reads X-Correlation-ID from the incoming request — or generates a UUID if it's absent — and puts it into MDC. Since we use logstash-logback-encoder, every subsequent log.info() on that thread automatically includes the correlation ID in the JSON output. For outgoing HTTP calls, we have a RestTemplate interceptor that reads the ID from MDC and adds it as a header. The downstream service repeats the process. In Kibana, you search for correlationId:"abc123" and see the complete request chain across all servers in chronological order. The main gotcha is async processing — MDC doesn't cross thread boundaries, so we use a task decorator on our thread pools to copy the MDC map to the new thread.
Saying "we use distributed tracing" without explaining the concrete mechanism — correlation ID, MDC, header propagation.
Intermediate "What would you monitor for a Spring Boot microservice — walk me through your checklist."

Tests breadth — do you go beyond CPU/memory to business metrics and JVM internals?

  • Four golden signals: latency (p99), traffic (req/s), errors (5xx rate), saturation (CPU, threads)
  • JVM: heap usage, GC pause times, thread pool states, connection pool utilization
  • Business KPIs: domain-specific counters (orders placed, payments failed)
  • External dependencies: downstream HTTP latency and error rates, circuit breaker state
  • Queue health: consumer lag, DLQ depth
I start with the four golden signals from Google SRE — latency at p95 and p99, request rate, 5xx error rate, and saturation. For saturation, I watch both CPU and thread pool utilization, since thread pool exhaustion is often what kills a Java service before CPU does. Beyond that: JVM heap and GC pause times because a GC pause over 500ms shows up as a latency spike in your p99. HikariCP connection pool — near-full pools cause requests to queue before you hit any errors. Then business metrics: for an order service that means orders placed per minute and payment failure rate. Those catch problems that infra metrics never will. Finally, any external dependencies — their latency and error rate, and whether circuit breakers are open. In Spring Boot I wire all of this through Micrometer so it exports automatically to Prometheus.
Stopping at CPU, memory, and HTTP error rate without mentioning JVM internals, business KPIs, or dependency health.
Senior "When would you choose Loki over Elasticsearch for log storage, and what are the trade-offs?"

Tests architectural trade-off thinking — is there a principled reason for the choice, or just "we used what we knew"?

  • Elasticsearch indexes every field — powerful ad-hoc queries, much higher storage and CPU cost
  • Loki indexes only labels — far cheaper, but you must filter by labels before scanning log bodies
  • Query pattern drives the choice: ad-hoc investigation → Elasticsearch, time+service filter → Loki
  • Loki integrates naturally with Grafana/Prometheus stack — consistent tooling
The decision comes down to query patterns and cost. Elasticsearch indexes every JSON field, so you can search by any field at any time — great for ad-hoc investigation where you don't know in advance what you'll need to query. But that indexing is expensive: storage costs are significantly higher and it needs substantial resources to run. Loki only indexes label values like service name, environment, and log level — the log body is stored compressed. So you query by labels first to narrow down the set, then scan the body. If your team's workflow is always "filter by service and time range, then search the text" — which is most teams — Loki works great and costs far less. The other factor is ecosystem: if you're already on Grafana + Prometheus, Loki fits naturally. If you need full-text search across arbitrary fields or you're doing security analysis, Elasticsearch is the right tool.
Saying "Elasticsearch is better" or "Loki is better" without articulating the query pattern and cost trade-off that drives the choice.
Senior "How do you prevent alert fatigue in a team? What's your philosophy on alerting?"

Tests maturity and culture — purely technical engineers often miss this. It's as much about process as tooling.

  • Only alert on things that require human action — if no action, it should be a dashboard, not an alert
  • Use the for: clause to require conditions to persist before firing
  • Alert on symptoms (user-facing impact), not causes (high CPU isn't a user problem yet)
  • Treat every alert that fires without action as a bug — it should be deleted, raised, or fixed
My rule is: if a page doesn't require someone to do something right now, it shouldn't page. Alert fatigue happens when on-call gets trained to ignore alerts because half of them are noise. Every alert that fires and gets silenced without action is a false alarm that erodes trust in the system. Practically: I alert on symptoms over causes — "5% of requests are failing" is a symptom, "CPU is at 80%" is a cause that might resolve itself. I always use the for: clause in Prometheus rules so transient spikes don't fire. And we do a monthly alert audit: any alert that fired in the last month without generating an incident is either deleted or its threshold is revisited. The goal is that when an alert fires, the on-call engineer trusts that it's real and acts immediately.
Talking only about tooling (Alertmanager, PagerDuty routing) without addressing the culture and process of alert maintenance.
